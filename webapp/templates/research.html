Why These Patterns?

Plain‑English notes on where they come from.

Deterministic Patterns

PromptKit favors small, deterministic rules. This keeps fixes explainable and reusable across models.

Constraint Ledger

Inspired by checklists and confirmation practices. Prevents contradictions by echoing include/avoid/not_too/vibes before action.

Contrastive Clarify

One either/or clarifier comes from repair strategies in conversation design. Reduces guessing, keeps momentum.

Exemplar Propose

Using tiny concrete options anchors vague requests—similar to design probes and A/B choice framing.

Override Hook

Operational control pattern: quick commands (override/lock/reduce/reset) for fast, explicit corrections.

State Bag

Keep explicit state (goal/include/avoid/not_too/memory/next_step/confirmed). Echo updates and confirm before finalizing. Borrowed from control loops and recap checklists.

Slot Filling

Classic dialog “form filling”: define required slots, ask only for missing, never re‑ask captured ones. Summarize and confirm.

Related Research & Frameworks

  • Dialog systems: slot filling, confirmation/repair strategies
  • Checklists & control loops: PDCA (Plan‑Do‑Check‑Act), recap & confirm
  • Software practices: preconditions, output contracts, TDD‑style pass criteria
  • Reasoning frameworks: Chain‑of‑Thought (CoT), Tree‑of‑Thought (ToT), ReAct, Reflexion
  • Programmatic guards: schema‑first generation (e.g., output contracts)

Model Considerations (Advisory)

  • Prefer short, atomic rules; avoid chained instructions
  • Echo state/constraints briefly; confirm before finalizing
  • Use clear section headings to enforce output shape

These notes are informational. PromptKit patterns remain deterministic and model‑agnostic by design.
